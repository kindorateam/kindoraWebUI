What you have
Your backend Room model (and the FE types mirroring it) stores per room: capacity, ratio (a single number), minAge/maxAge in months, status, logo, color. Every record in the system is scoped to a companyId (that's the multi-tenancy — one database serving many daycare businesses, with tenantWhere() making sure company A can never query company B's children). And routes check permissions like authorize(currentUser, "rooms:move") — that's the role-based access control. This is a genuinely good foundation; most of it the memo lists as table stakes.

What's missing, and why it matters
Program type. Georgia licenses two different things — child care learning centers (CCLC) and family child care learning homes (FCCLH) — under two different rulebooks (different ratios, records, staffing rules). Your data model has no field saying which kind of program a site is, so there's nowhere to hang "this rule applies to centers only." The memo says: model it from day one, because retrofitting "which rules apply to this customer" later touches everything.

Operating hours/holidays. No model for "we're open 6:30–18:30, closed these dates." That feeds late-pickup fees, attendance expectations, billing proration, staff scheduling — small field, many dependents.

The ratio problem — the core of the warning. Today a room stores ratio: 10, meaning "1 staff per 10 children." But look at the actual Georgia rule your product would need to enforce:

Age group	Ratio	Max group
Infants < 1yr	1:6	12
1yr walking	1:8	16
2yr	1:10	20
3yr	1:15	30
4yr	1:18	36
5yr	1:20	40
6+	1:25	50
Three things a single per-room number can't express:

The ratio is a function of who's in the room, not of the room. If a 2-year-old walks into the 3-year-olds' room, the required ratio for that room changes (mixed-age groups generally take the strictest applicable ratio). With ratio: 15 stored on the room, your "live ratio dashboard" would happily show green while the center is actually out of compliance — the worst possible failure for a product whose pitch is "inspection-ready."
There's a second constraint you don't store at all: max group size. A room can satisfy 1:15 with 30 kids and 2 teachers and still violate the 30-child group cap... actually meet it exactly — at 31 it violates even with 3 teachers. Ratio and group size are independent limits.
Rules are state-specific and change over time. Tennessee's infant ratio isn't Georgia's; Georgia itself can amend the table (the current rules doc is literally versioned "7-1-25"). If ratio logic is hard-coded — whether as one number per room or one Georgia table in the source — expanding to state #2, or surviving a rule amendment, means a code rewrite and, worse, historical reports become wrong ("were we compliant last March?" needs last March's rules).
What the memo prescribes instead — the "age-band rule engine with versioned state rules" — is a small set of tables roughly like:


RuleSet:   state=GA, programType=CCLC, effectiveFrom=2025-07-01
RatioRule: ruleSet → ageBand(0–12mo) → ratio 1:6, maxGroup 12
RatioRule: ruleSet → ageBand(24–36mo) → ratio 1:10, maxGroup 20
...
Then "required staff for room X right now" is computed: take the children currently present (you already have this via roomPresence + birth dates), find each child's age band, apply the strictest rule, compare against staff currently clocked into that room. The room's own ratio field becomes at most a display default. Adding a state = inserting rows, not shipping code; a rule change = a new effectiveFrom version, and old reports still evaluate against the rules that were in force.

Why the memo is emphatic about this: for the "Georgia-compliance-native" positioning, the ratio engine is the product. It's also the one thing that's cheap to design correctly now and expensive to bolt on later — your current schema would need birthDate-driven age banding, a rules table, and presence-vs-clock-in joins either way; doing it after you've built dashboards, alerts, and historical reports on top of room.ratio means rebuilding all of them.

The practical takeaway isn't "rip out Room.ratio today" — it's: when you build the live-ratio/alerting feature, build it as computed from children present + versioned rules, not read from that field.

What's new (the small parts):

Two tiny tables + a seed — RatioRuleSet (state, programType, effectiveFrom) and RatioRule (ageBand min/max months, ratio, maxGroupSize). Seed one row set: the Georgia CCLC table. One Prisma migration.
Two columns — state and programType on Company (or Room). For now every customer is GA/CCLC anyway, so they can even default.
One computation function + endpoint — for each room: children present → birth dates → age bands → strictest applicable rule → required staff; compare to staff present. ~50 lines of controller logic joining data you already collect.
FE rooms dashboard — swap "static ratio number" for "required X / present Y" + a violation badge. The room cards already render live counts, so it's a display change, not a new page.
Room.ratio stays as-is — it just stops being the source of truth for compliance and becomes a display default. No existing feature breaks.

Realistic size: a few days, not weeks. The two genuine wrinkles:

Children without birthDate (it's optional in your schema) — need a policy: fall back to the room's minAge, or flag the child as "can't evaluate." Flagging is more honest for a compliance product.
Historical ratio log ("prove we were compliant in March") — that needs presence events with timestamps, not just current state. The memo's warning isn't "this is a big build" — it's "don't build dashboards on top of the wrong field and then pay to redo them." Building the engine first, while nothing depends on room.ratio, is exactly the cheap moment.

On operating hours/holidays — agreed, defer it. It's a billing/late-fee concern with no compliance dependency. Only nuance: when you do billing, budget for it then, since proration and late-pickup fees both want it.
